SECTION 04
A short history

Older than America.
Older than English.
Concrete works.

The Pantheon in Rome was finished around 126 A.D. The dome — the largest unreinforced concrete dome in the world, then or now — is still up there. It has survived earthquakes, sieges, lightning strikes, two thousand winters, and approximately nine million tourists a year. Nobody has had to repour it. Nobody has had to reinforce it. Hadrian's contractors got it right the first time.

Concrete is not a new idea. Concrete is the oldest serious idea humans have ever had about building things that last. We did not invent it. We just use it strategically.

126 A.D.
The Pantheon
Rome. Concrete dome, 142 feet across. Still standing. Still in use. 1,900 years and counting.
~80 A.D.
The Colosseum
Roman concrete frame. The most photographed building on earth. Older than every flag in the room.
22 B.C.
The Caesarea seawalls
Roman marine concrete, submerged in salt water for two millennia. Still intact. Florida is paying attention.
1853
Coignet's house
Saint-Denis, France. The first reinforced concrete house. Listed as a historic monument. Still here.
1891
Court Street
Bellefontaine, Ohio. The first concrete street in America. Tested at 8,000 PSI. Still being driven on.
1903
The Ingalls Building
Cincinnati. World's first concrete skyscraper. A reporter waited up all night for it to fall. It's a hotel now.

"Modern codes of engineering practice would not permit such mischief." — David Moore, on the Pantheon. He meant it as a compliment.

A Tooly is built from poured concrete. Hurricane-rated. Sealed. Reinforced. Designed to outlive its first owner, its second owner, and probably its third. It is not a tiny home. It is not a trailer. It is the same idea Hadrian had, with better insulation and a working dishwasher.

How we landed on this number. Florida's median household income is $77,700. The federal definition of affordable housing caps rent at 30% of income, which for a Florida household at AMI is roughly $1,943 a month. Toolys typically rent in the $1,500–$1,900 band — comfortably affordable for a working-class tenant, comfortably profitable for the owner.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Density without gentrification.

Every other answer to the housing crisis involves tearing down what's already there. A developer buys a working-class block, knocks it flat, builds a five-over-one with a yoga studio on the ground floor, and the people who lived there move thirty miles away.

Toolys go in existing backyards on existing lots owned by existing homeowners. Nobody gets displaced. The block stays the block. The supply goes up. The rent goes down. The kid who fixed your AC last August can still afford to live three blocks from his mother.
